# English Grammar Roadmap

> A CEFR-aligned (A1 → C2) field guide to English grammar, built on Cambridge's **English Grammar Profile** and enriched with multiple authoritative references — **arranged in pedagogical order**, not alphabetical.

---

## What this is

The [**English Grammar Profile (EGP)**](https://englishprofile.org/?menu=egp-online) is a research output from Cambridge English. Linguists analysed the *Cambridge Learner Corpus* — millions of words written by learners taking Cambridge exams — and identified **1,218 grammatical structures** that real learners produce reliably at each CEFR level. The result is a level-by-level *can-do* statement for every grammar point that matters.

This roadmap takes that EGP map as a spine and re-organises it into **17 numbered chapters** that follow how grammar is actually taught: tenses first (they carry most of English), then word classes (nouns → articles → pronouns → adjectives → adverbs → prepositions), then verb behaviour (patterns, modality, passives), then sentence shape (questions, negation, conjunctions, clauses), and finally the advanced layer (reported speech, focus & emphasis, discourse markers).

- **Every one of the 1,218 EGP structures** appears with its can-do statement, level, examples, and common-mistake guidance.
- **Each entry is enriched** with form description, meaning notes, register notes, and BrE/AmE contrasts drawn from at least two of: *Cambridge Dictionary Grammar*, Swan's *Practical English Usage*, Murphy's *English Grammar in Use*, Hewings' *Advanced Grammar in Use*, BBC Learning English, British Council, Oxford Learner's.
- **`find-errors.md`** in every chapter folder turns the EGP "Incorrect" examples into spot-the-mistake exercises with a collapsible answer key.
- **Supplementary topics** beyond EGP (punctuation rules, register, formal-letter conventions, emerging grammar like singular `they`) are added under each chapter README's `## Supplementary (beyond EGP)` heading.

## How the roadmap is organised

### Two complementary entry points

**1 · Chapter-first (recommended for sequential learning).** Walk the 17 numbered chapters in order. Each chapter is a folder; each contains a README, six level files (`A1.md` → `C2.md`, only those that have entries), and a `find-errors.md`.

**2 · Level-first (for exam pacing).** Use the inverse view in `levels/`. Each `levels/<LEVEL>.md` lists every entry at that CEFR level grouped by chapter, with anchor links into the chapter folders.

**One file per sub-topic, levels inside.** Inside each sub-topic file, the EGP entries are grouped under `## A1`, `## A2`, … `## C2`, so you read one grammar topic from beginner to advanced in a single page. The sub-topic file is the unit of reading; the chapter folder is the unit of organisation.

## Recommended learning path

### Sequential — sub-topic by sub-topic, A1 first

The cleanest single-pass route. Open a sub-topic file (e.g. `01-tenses/01-present-simple.md`); read it from `## A1` down to whatever level you can handle; move on to the next sub-topic file.

| Step | Focus | Approx time | Why |
|:---:|:---|:---:|:---|
| 1 | **Chapter 1 — Tenses** sub-topics 1.1 → 1.8 | 8–12 wk | Tenses are the spine. Get the seven core tenses solid before anything else: present simple, present continuous, past simple, past continuous, present perfect simple/continuous, past perfect simple/continuous. |
| 2 | **Chapter 1 — Tenses** sub-topics 1.9 → 1.17 | 4–6 wk | Future and future-in-past forms. |
| 3 | **Chapters 2–7** *(Nouns → Adverbs → Prepositions)* | 6–10 wk | Word classes. By the end you can describe people, places, routines. |
| 4 | **Chapter 8 — Verb Patterns** | 2–3 wk | `to`-infinitive vs. `-ing`; `there is/are`; phrasal verbs. |
| 5 | **Chapter 9 — Modality** | 4–6 wk | The longest chapter (238 entries). `can`, `could`, `must`, `should`, `would`, `will`, `may`, `might`, perfects. |
| 6 | **Chapters 10–14** | 4–6 wk | Passives, questions, negation, conjunctions, clauses. |
| 7 | **Chapters 15–17** | 2–4 wk | Reported speech, focus & emphasis, discourse markers. |

Inside any sub-topic file you can stop at the level that matches you (A1, A2, …) and come back later for the higher levels.

### By-level — for exam preparation

Open `levels/A1.md` (or whichever level matches your exam) and walk every entry top to bottom. Each entry links into its sub-topic file for deeper reading.

> **Tip — the 80/20 of English grammar:** chapters 1 (Tenses, 203), 9 (Modality, 238), 14 (Clauses, 135), and 4 (Pronouns, 118) together account for **57 % of EGP**. If a structure feels hard, it is statistically likely to live in one of those four chapters.

## How each entry is structured

Every grammar point — at every level, in every sub-topic file — uses the same block (full spec in `TEMPLATE.md`):

```markdown
### {LEVEL}.{i} — {short title in Sentence case}

> **Can-do (CEFR {Level}):** {definition verbatim from EGP}

**Description**   — at least 5 sentences, in this order:
                    1. what the structure is
                    2. what it means
                    3. when to use it
                    4. what contrasts with it
                    5. a pitfall, register caveat, or BrE/AmE note

**Form**          — structure, agreement, position, exceptions
**Examples**
✅ Correct        — numbered list (≥ 2)
❌ Common mistakes — numbered list with "why it's wrong" (≥ 1)
**Related**       — links to neighbouring entries inside the roadmap
**Sources**       — at least two: EGP + ≥1 of Cambridge Grammar / Swan / Murphy / Hewings / BBC / British Council / Oxford Learner's
```

The numbering inside a sub-topic file uses the **CEFR level** as the prefix (e.g. `### A1.1`, `### A2.5`, `### B1.3`), so the entry is self-locating: you always know the level without reading the can-do block. For C1 / C2 entries (241 in total) the **Description** section is expanded to cover register, pragmatic appropriateness, and contrastive notes — the "advanced grammar" depth that EGP itself does not provide.

## Sources & citation policy

EGP gives the *map* — which structure belongs to which level, with one canonical can-do statement and a short example list. It does **not** give complete grammatical explanations, exhaustive contrast notes, register guidance, or modern usage commentary. So every entry is built from at least two sources, with EGP as one of them.

**Tier 1 — primary references** *(cite at least one per entry beyond EGP)*

- [Cambridge Dictionary Grammar (British)](https://dictionary.cambridge.org/grammar/british-grammar/)
- *Practical English Usage* — Michael Swan (4th ed., OUP)
- *English Grammar in Use* — Raymond Murphy (Cambridge)
- *Advanced Grammar in Use* — Martin Hewings (Cambridge)
- [Oxford Learner's Dictionaries — Grammar](https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/grammar)

**Tier 2 — corpus & teaching commentary**

- [English Profile — EGP Online](https://englishprofile.org/?menu=egp-online)
- [Cambridge ELT Blog — Introducing the EGP](https://www.cambridge.org/elt/blog/2015/11/16/introducing-english-grammar-profile-2-whats-use-egp/)
- [BBC Learning English — Grammar](https://www.bbc.co.uk/learningenglish/english/)
- [British Council — LearnEnglish Grammar](https://learnenglish.britishcouncil.org/grammar)
- [Purdue OWL — Grammar](https://owl.purdue.edu/) *(American usage / contrast notes)*

**Tier 3 — focused topical references**

- [Englicious / UCL Survey of English Usage](https://www.englicious.org/) — terminological precision
- *The Cambridge Grammar of the English Language* — Huddleston & Pullum — for theoretical depth in C1/C2 entries

**Rules of engagement**

1. The `Form` and `Meaning & Use` sections are **paraphrased** from sources, never copy-pasted. Each entry's `Sources:` line lists what was actually consulted (≥ 2).
2. Where authoritative sources disagree with EGP — on level placement, terminology, or acceptability — both views are recorded with a `> **Note:**` admonition.
3. **Supplementary topics not in EGP** (punctuation of relative clauses, hedging in academic register, BrE/AmE contrast on `have got`, formal-letter conventions, singular `they`) are added under each chapter README's `## Supplementary (beyond EGP)` heading, with their own citations.
4. AmE / BrE differences are flagged inline (`🇬🇧 BrE` / `🇺🇸 AmE`) wherever they materially affect form or use.
