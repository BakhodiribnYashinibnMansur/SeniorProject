# Present simple — B2

> The present simple stops being just a tense and becomes a **stylistic tool**. It powers narrative, commentary, formal performatives, and emphatic inversion.

## 1 · What's new at B2

By B2 the rules are second nature. Now you choose the present simple for **effect**, not just because it's grammatically correct. The level adds:

1. The **historical (narrative) present** — telling stories vividly.
2. **Performative verbs** in formal and written register.
3. **Wider speech-act verbs** — *agree, disagree, accept, advise, claim, deny, warn, confirm, assure, declare*.
4. **Sports and live commentary**.
5. **Emphatic inversion** with *Not only … but also …*.
6. The **register choice** between past simple and historical present.

---

## 2 · The historical present — narrative tense for vivid storytelling

When you summarise a film, retell a novel, recount a joke, or describe a historical event, English often switches to the **present simple** even though the events happened in the past. This is called the *historical* (or *narrative*) present.

It pulls the reader **into** the action — the events feel like they're unfolding now.

> *In the opening scene, Hamlet **meets** the ghost of his father and **vows** revenge.*
> *Napoleon **invades** Russia in 1812 and **loses** half his army before reaching Moscow.*
> *So this guy **walks** into a bar and **says** to the barman …*

You can mix it with the **present continuous** for ongoing scene-setting and embed past tenses for events that happened *before* the story:

> *The scene **takes** place on a train. A man **is sleeping**. He **dreams** of a colleague who **lost** his job last year.*

Where it differs from the past simple:

| Past simple | Present simple (historical) |
|---|---|
| *Hamlet **met** the ghost.* | *Hamlet **meets** the ghost.* |
| Distance — recap from outside | Immediacy — tour through the action |
| Most academic, formal essays | Plot summaries, recipes, jokes, sports |

The trap is **mixing tenses** — once you're in the historical present, stay there:

> ❌ *The hero **meets** a stranger and they **took** the train together.*
> ✅ *The hero **meets** a stranger and they **take** the train together.*

## 3 · Performative verbs — words that do the deed

A **performative verb** *performs* the action it names when uttered. Saying *I promise* is the promise. These verbs go in the **present simple, first person**, often in formal or written register.

| Verb | Use | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **declare** | formal announcement | *I **declare** the meeting open.* |
| **pronounce** | give a verdict, ruling | *I **pronounce** you husband and wife.* |
| **resign** | quit | *I **resign** from my position.* |
| **object** | formally disagree | *I **object** to that statement.* |
| **insist** | demand firmly | *I **insist** on a refund.* |
| **confirm** | make official | *I **confirm** that the order has been processed.* |
| **assure** | give confidence | *I **assure** you that everything is fine.* |
| **swear / vow** | solemn promise | *I **swear** I'll be there.* |
| **propose** | put forward formally | *I **propose** we vote on the matter.* |
| **second** | support a proposal | *I **second** the motion.* |

These are typical of business letters, legal documents, ceremonies, formal meetings:

> *I **hereby confirm** receipt of your invoice.*
> *I **assure** you of our continued cooperation.*
> *I **resign** from the committee with immediate effect.*

## 4 · Wider speech-act verbs

At B1 you had `apologise · promise · suggest · recommend · agree · accept · invite · thank`. At B2, add:

| Verb | Pattern | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **disagree** | *with + noun / that-clause* | *I **disagree** with the new policy.* |
| **accept** | *noun / that-clause* | *I **accept** that I was wrong.* |
| **advise** | *object + to + verb* / *against + -ing* | *I **advise** you to take a coat.* |
| **claim** | *that-clause / noun* | *He **claims** that he saw the accident.* |
| **deny** | *-ing / that-clause* | *She **denies** taking the money.* |
| **warn** | *object + about + noun / + to + verb / + that-clause* | *I **warn** you about the road conditions.* |
| **confirm** | *that-clause / noun* | *We **confirm** your booking.* |
| **welcome** | *noun / object* | *I **welcome** the decision.* |
| **congratulate** | *object + on + -ing* | *I **congratulate** you on your promotion.* |

Each verb has its own **complementation pattern** — what kind of object or clause it takes. Mixing them up is the dominant B2 mistake:

> ❌ *I disagree to the proposal.*
> ✅ *I disagree **with** the proposal.*
> ❌ *I advise that you to take a coat.*
> ✅ *I advise you **to take** a coat.* / *I advise **that you take** a coat.*
> ❌ *He claims to see the accident.*
> ✅ *He claims **to have seen** the accident.* / *He claims **that he saw** the accident.*

## 5 · Live commentary, recipes, demonstrations

Sports commentary, cooking shows, and demonstration scripts run in the present simple to convey live action.

> *Messi **takes** the ball, **passes** to Ronaldo, **shoots** — and **scores**!*
> *Now I **add** the flour, **stir** for ten seconds, and **pour** the mixture into the tin.*
> *The patient **arrives**, the nurse **takes** their temperature, the doctor **examines** them.*

Like the historical present, this is a **register** choice that creates immediacy. The same actions could be described in the past simple if you were recounting them later — but live, present is the convention.

## 6 · Emphatic inversion with *Not only … but also*

When you put **`Not only`** at the start of a sentence, English requires **subject-auxiliary inversion** — exactly like a question.

> ***Not only does** the new system **save** time, **but it also reduces** errors.*
> ***Not only do** they **offer** great food, **but they also have** the best service in town.*
> ***Not only does** Tokyo **have** world-class food, **but it also has** stunning architecture.*

Without fronting, the sentence is normal:

> *The new system not only **saves** time but also **reduces** errors.*

The inversion is **formal** and **written**. In casual speech, speakers usually keep the standard order. The mistake to watch: forgetting the helper after the front.

> ❌ *Not only **the new system saves** time, but also reduces errors.*
> ✅ *Not only **does the new system save** time, but it also reduces errors.*

Same trick can fire with other auxiliaries:

> ***Not only is** he late, **but he is also** unprepared.*
> ***Not only has** she finished the report, **but she has also** sent it to the client.*

(More fronting/inversion patterns at C1 and C2 — see those pages.)

## 7 · Choosing past simple vs historical present

You now have two viable tenses for narrating past events. The choice is **register**, not grammar.

| Use the past simple … | Use the historical present … |
|---|---|
| In academic essays | In plot summaries |
| In formal historical writing | In jokes and anecdotes |
| When you want **distance** | When you want **immediacy** |
| When recounting from outside | When taking the reader inside the action |
| News reports of past events | Sports and live commentary |
| Personal memoirs | Recipe demonstrations |

A book review usually mixes both:

> *Tolstoy **wrote** *War and Peace* in the 1860s. The novel **opens** in St Petersburg in 1805. Pierre Bezukhov **enters** society and **falls** for Hélène …*

The first sentence is **past simple** — biographical fact. The rest is **historical present** — plot summary.

## 8 · Common B2 mistakes

| ❌ Wrong | ✅ Right |
|---|---|
| *The hero meets a stranger and they took the train.* | The hero **meets** a stranger and they **take** the train. |
| *Not only they save time, but reduce errors.* | **Not only do they save** time, but they also reduce errors. |
| *Not only do they saves time …* | Not only do they **save** time …  |
| *I disagree to the new system.* | I disagree **with** the new system. |
| *I advise that you to take a coat.* | I advise **you to take** a coat. |
| *He claims to see the accident.* | He **claims that he saw** / **claims to have seen** the accident. |
| *She denies to take the money.* | She **denies taking** the money. |
| *I welcome to the decision.* | I **welcome** the decision. |
| *The film starts and a man was sleeping.* | The film **starts** and a man **is sleeping**. |
| *I am suggesting we leave.* (suggestion) | I **suggest** we leave. |
