## 1 · What's new at A2

At A1 you learned the basic shape and a couple of uses. At A2 you expand in five directions:

1. More irregular verbs and spelling rules.
2. **Wh-** questions (`what · where · when · who · why · which · how`).
3. **Polite indirect questions** with *Do you know …?*
4. The **first conditional** with `if`.
5. **Instructions, directions, and suggestions** in everyday speech.
6. Talking about **thoughts, hopes, and likes** with stative verbs.

---

## 2 · Spelling rules for the `-s` form

You met `play → plays` at A1. Now learn the small tweaks for less regular verbs:

| Ending | Rule | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| consonant + `-y` | change `-y` to `-ies` | *study → studies · try → tries · cry → cries · carry → carries* |
| vowel + `-y` | just add `-s` | *play → plays · enjoy → enjoys · buy → buys* |
| `-s, -sh, -ch, -x, -z` | add `-es` | *miss → misses · wash → washes · watch → watches · fix → fixes* |
| `-o` | add `-es` | *go → goes · do → does · echo → echoes* |
| irregular | memorise | *be → is · have → has* |

> *He **studies** medicine.*
> *She **watches** the news every evening.*
> *My brother **does** his homework after dinner.*
> *The story **goes** like this …*

## 3 · `Wh-` questions

Put a question word at the front, then `do / does + subject + base verb`.

| Question word | Asks about | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **what** | thing | *What **do you like**?* |
| **where** | place | *Where **does he live**?* |
| **when** | time | *When **do they finish**?* |
| **why** | reason | *Why **do you study** English?* |
| **how** | manner | *How **does this work**?* |
| **how often** | frequency | *How often **do you go** out?* |
| **how long** | duration | *How long **does the journey take**?* |
| **which** | choice | *Which **do you prefer**?* |

> *Where **do you come from**?*
> *What **does she do** for a living?*
> *How **do you spell** your name?*
> *Why **does he always arrive** late?*

When `who` or `what` is the **subject** of the question, drop the helper:

> *Who **knows** the answer?* (no `do`)
> *What **happens** at the end?* (no `does`)

But when they are the **object**, keep the helper:

> *Who **do you like**?* — `you` is the subject, `who` is the object.
> *What **does she want**?* — `she` is the subject, `what` is the object.

## 4 · Polite indirect questions: *Do you know …?*

Wrap a question inside *Do you know …?* to sound polite. The trick: inside the wrapper, English uses **statement order** (subject before verb), not question order.

> ***Do you know where the station is**?* — not *Do you know where is the station?*
> ***Do you know how I get** to your house?* — not *Do you know how do I get …?*
> ***Do you know what time it starts**?* — not *Do you know what time does it start?*

For yes/no embedded questions, use **`if`** or **`whether`**:

> *Do you know **if it is open**?*
> *I'm not sure **whether she works** today.*

Same pattern with *Could you tell me …?*, *I wonder …*, *Can you tell me …?*

> *Could you tell me **where the bank is**?*
> *I wonder **what she wants**.*

## 5 · The first conditional — *If + present simple, will + verb*

Talk about **real, possible** situations in the future. The verb in the `if`-clause goes in the **present simple**, even though the situation is in the future.

> ***If you wait**, I'll help you.*
> ***If she calls**, tell her I'm out.*
> *I'll be late **if the train is delayed**.*

**Don't** put `will` in the `if`-clause:

> ❌ *If you **will wait**, I'll help you.*
> ✅ *If you **wait**, I'll help you.*

For things that are always true (zero conditional), use the present simple in **both** clauses:

> *If you **heat** ice, it **melts**.*
> *Plants **die** if you **don't water** them.*

## 6 · Instructions and directions

When you tell someone how to do something or how to get somewhere, English uses the present simple in the **second person** (`you`).

> *You **walk** to the corner, you **turn** left, and the bank **is** on your right.*
> *First you **crack** the egg, then you **whisk** it, then you **add** salt.*
> *To make tea, you **boil** the water, **put** the bag in, and **wait** three minutes.*

This is softer than the imperative *(Walk to the corner. Turn left.)* and is common in friendly conversation.

## 7 · Suggestions: *Why don't you …?*

A negative question that is really an offer.

| Pattern | Use | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Why don't you** + verb …? | suggest something for the listener | *Why don't you **take** a taxi?* |
| **Why don't we** + verb …? | suggest something for both of you | *Why don't we **eat out** tonight?* |
| *We can / could …* + **if you want / if you like** | softer suggestion | *We could **go** to the cinema, if you like.* |

> *Why don't you **try** the soup?*
> *Why don't we **meet** at six?*
> *We can **share** a taxi, if you want.*

The listener should reply with *Yes, good idea* or *No, I'd rather …*, not with a literal reason.

## 8 · Stative verbs — thoughts, hopes, likes, knowledge

Some verbs describe **states of mind**, not actions. They go in the present simple, **never** in the present continuous.

| Verb | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **think** | hold an opinion | *I **think** you're right.* |
| **hope** | want something to be true | *I **hope** you feel better.* |
| **know** | have information | *Do you **know** her?* |
| **want** | wish for | *I **want** a coffee.* |
| **need** | require | *We **need** more time.* |
| **like / love / hate** | feeling | *She **loves** chocolate.* |
| **prefer** | choose A over B | *I **prefer** tea to coffee.* |
| **understand** | grasp the meaning | *Do you **understand**?* |

> ✅ *I **think** it's a good idea.*
> ❌ *I'm thinking it's a good idea.* (in this meaning)
> ✅ *She **loves** her job.*
> ❌ *She is loving her job.*

Negative thoughts usually go on the main verb:

> ✅ *I **don't think** it's true.* (natural)
> ❌ *I think it isn't true.* (less natural)

## 9 · More frequency adverbs

Add to your A1 list:

| Adverb | Frequency |
|---|---|
| always | 100% |
| **usually / normally** | most of the time |
| **often** | many times |
| sometimes | occasionally |
| **rarely / seldom** | almost never |
| **hardly ever** | almost never (informal) |
| never | 0% |

Position rules same as A1:

- Before the main verb: *I **usually** walk to work.*
- After `be`: *He **is rarely** late.*

> *We **often** have guests at weekends.*
> *I **hardly ever** drink soda.*
> *She **is usually** in bed by ten.*

## 10 · Common A2 mistakes

| ❌ Wrong | ✅ Right |
|---|---|
| *Where do he live?* | Where **does** he live? |
| *Does she likes coffee?* | Does she **like** coffee? |
| *Where lives he?* | Where **does he live**? |
| *Do you know where is the station?* | Do you know **where the station is**? |
| *If you will come, I will help.* | **If you come,** I will help. |
| *I am thinking you are right.* | I **think** you are right. |
| *Why you don't take a taxi?* | **Why don't you** take a taxi? |
| *She studys medicine.* | She **studies** medicine. |
| *He gos to school.* | He **goes** to school. |
| *I don't never go out.* | I **never** go out. |

## 11 · Practice — try writing these

1. Write three things you **usually** do at the weekend.
2. Ask a friend five `wh-` questions about their life.
3. Wrap one of those questions inside *Do you know …?*
4. Write one first-conditional sentence about tomorrow.
5. Give directions from your home to the nearest shop.
6. Suggest two things to a friend using *Why don't we …?*
7. Say three things you **think**, **hope**, or **want**.

If you can do all seven, move on to [B1](B1.md).
