# Present simple — C2

> Native-like control. By C2 you switch the present simple in and out of a sentence for **stylistic effect** — emphasis, archaism, headline economy, idiomatic flair.

[siblings: A1](A1.md) · [A2](A2.md) · [B1](B1.md) · [B2](B2.md) · [C1](C1.md) · [tricky](tricky.md) · [find errors](find-errors.md)

---

## 1 · What's new at C2

Almost no new grammar. What is new is **stylistic awareness** — knowing what register an unusual present-simple sentence carries, and using or avoiding it deliberately.

1. **Emphatic `do`** for emphasis or contrast.
2. **Headline grammar** — the present simple in newspaper headlines and signs.
3. **Fixed expressions and idioms** locked into the present simple.
4. **Existential and inverted patterns** with *here / there / now*.
5. **Performative archaisms** in legal, ceremonial, and religious register.
6. **Free indirect speech** and the **truisms** of literary register.

---

## 2 · Emphatic `do` — *I do think she's right*

Insert an unstressed `do` / `does` before a present-simple verb to add **emphasis** or **contrast**.

> *I **do think** she's right.* (= I really, genuinely think so)
> *He **does work** hard, you know.* (defending him)
> *She **does have** a point.* (conceding)
> *They **do tend** to overreact.* (mild concession)

Three contexts where emphatic `do` fires naturally:

| Context | Example |
|---|---|
| Disagreement with an implied claim | — *You don't care.* — *I **do care**.* |
| Concession before a counter-argument | *He **does** work hard, but the results are mixed.* |
| Reinforcing agreement | *I **do** think we should leave.* |

In writing, italicise the `do` in informal style: *I **do** think she's right.*

Common C2 fluency mistake: overusing emphatic `do` until it sounds robotic or pleading.

## 3 · Headline grammar — the news-headline present

Newspaper headlines compress past, present, and future events into the present simple. The convention is **economy** — fewer letters, more punch.

| Headline | Full sentence (past) |
|---|---|
| *Plane **crashes** in mountains.* | *A plane **crashed** in the mountains.* |
| *PM **announces** new policy.* | *The PM **announced** a new policy.* |
| *Protestors **march** through capital.* | *Protestors **marched** through the capital.* |

The same convention powers signs, captions, and labels:

> *No smoking. · Mind the gap. · Push to open. · Beware of the dog.*

These are not full grammar — they are register conventions. A C2 user **recognises** them and does not import them into normal writing:

> ❌ *Yesterday plane crashes in mountains.* (mixing headlinese with ordinary sentence)
> ✅ *Yesterday a plane **crashed** in the mountains.*

## 4 · Fixed expressions and idioms

A handful of high-frequency expressions are **locked** into the present simple regardless of when they happen.

| Expression | Use |
|---|---|
| *Here **comes** the bus.* | something arriving now |
| *There **goes** my chance.* | something gone now |
| *Here **lies** … (on a tomb)* | epitaph, archaic |
| *Long **live** the king.* | ceremonial |
| *Easy **come**, easy **go**.* | proverb |
| *Time **flies**.* | proverb |
| *Birds of a feather **flock** together.* | proverb |
| *Practice **makes** perfect.* | proverb |
| *It **takes** two to tango.* | proverb |
| *What **goes** around **comes** around.* | proverb |

Inversion is built into many — *here comes*, *there goes*, *here lies* — and the present-simple form is fossilised.

## 5 · Existential and inverted patterns with `here / there / now`

Front a present-simple verb after `here`, `there`, or `now` for stylistic effect.

> ***Here comes** trouble.*
> ***There goes** another opportunity.*
> ***Now begins** the real work.*

The pattern is **subject-verb inversion** triggered by the front locative. It is **conversational and literary**, not academic.

> *Here **comes** the train!* (a child's storybook tone)
> *There **stands** the lonely lighthouse.* (descriptive prose)

Avoid in business or technical writing.

## 6 · Performative archaisms — legal, ceremonial, religious

Some performatives keep an old-fashioned register. They sit in the present simple but feel formal or ritualistic.

| Verb / pattern | Use |
|---|---|
| *I **hereby** declare / certify / confirm …* | legal, certificates |
| *I, [name], **do solemnly swear** that …* | oaths |
| *We **the undersigned** agree that …* | contracts |
| *I **do** take thee to be my lawful wedded wife.* | wedding vows |
| *I **pronounce** you …* | officiant statements |
| *Be it **resolved** that …* | parliamentary motions |
| *The court **finds** the defendant …* | legal judgments |

These survive precisely because they sound official. Using them in casual register sounds parodic.

> ✅ *I hereby resign with effect from 1 January.* (formal letter)
> ❌ *I hereby agree to meet you for coffee.* (over the top)

## 7 · Free indirect speech and truisms

In literary writing, the present simple slips into a character's perspective without using *says* / *thinks*. The narrator presents the character's thoughts as if they were facts.

> *He stares at the ceiling. Life **is** strange. People **come** and **go**, and nothing really **changes**.*

The present-simple sentences (*Life is strange · People come and go*) are the character's truisms — generalisations the character believes. The reader feels the perspective shift without explicit speech tags.

This is a literary device, not a grammar rule. C2 readers **recognise** it; C2 writers **deploy** it sparingly.

---

## 8 · Register summary — what register does my sentence carry?

The same form (present simple) carries different registers depending on context. A C2 user reads register fluently:

| Sentence | Register |
|---|---|
| *I work in a hospital.* | neutral, conversational |
| *The data demonstrate a correlation.* | academic, hedged |
| *Plane crashes in mountains.* | newspaper headline |
| *Long live the king.* | ceremonial |
| *Here comes trouble.* | conversational, expressive |
| *I do think she's right.* | emphatic, informal |
| *I hereby resign.* | formal letter |
| *He says nothing. Life **is** strange.* | literary, free indirect |

Choosing the wrong register is the C2 mistake — getting the grammar right but the **tone** wrong. *Here comes the executive summary* in a corporate report, or *I demonstrate that the policy is wrong* in an opinion column, both clash with their context.

## 9 · Common C2 mistakes

| ❌ Wrong / Off-register | ✅ Right |
|---|---|
| *I do really think.* (overuse of emphatic `do`) | *I **really do** think.* / *I really think.* |
| *Here is coming the bus.* | **Here comes** the bus. |
| *I hereby agree to lunch.* (over-formal) | I'd love to have lunch. |
| *Smith demonstrates that the data shows …* (mixed strength) | Smith **shows / suggests** that the data shows … |
| *Rarely I see this.* | **Rarely do I see** this. |
| *Were he to come, I would tell him.* (in casual chat) | **If he came**, I'd tell him. (register-appropriate) |
| *The president walks into the room and announced …* (tense mix) | walks … announces … (or walked … announced …) |
| *She do work hard.* (confused emphatic `do`) | She **does** work hard. |

## 10 · Practice

1. Take three of your own opinionated sentences. Add emphatic `do` to each. Read them aloud — do they sound natural?
2. Find five recent news headlines online. Convert each into a normal past-tense sentence.
3. Write three sentences using fronted *Here comes / There goes / Now begins*.
4. Draft a one-sentence formal performative for: a resignation, an oath, a meeting motion.
5. Write a short paragraph (4–5 sentences) of free indirect speech: a character thinking truisms in the present simple, narrated in third person.
6. Take the same idea and write it in three registers — academic, journalistic headline, conversational.

After this you have native-level control of the present simple. Move on to [Present continuous](../02-present-continuous/) when ready.
