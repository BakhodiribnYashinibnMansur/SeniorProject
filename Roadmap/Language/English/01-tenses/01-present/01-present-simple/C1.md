# Present simple — C1

> The present simple as the **default tense of academic prose** and the **fuel of rhetoric**. By C1 you control register through the present simple — formal, persuasive, hedged, or detached.

## 2 · Academic reporting verbs — pick the strength

In academic writing, the present simple is the default tense for **citing sources**, even when the source was written years ago. The verb stays present because the author's claim is still current.

The choice between verbs encodes how much you accept the source's claim. Match the verb to the strength you want.

| Verb | Strength | Use |
|---|---|---|
| **demonstrates** | very strong (= proves) | irrefutable evidence |
| **shows** | strong | clear evidence |
| **establishes** | strong | unchallenged fact |
| **confirms** | strong | matches earlier evidence |
| **reveals** | strong + dramatic | brings out something hidden |
| **argues** | partisan | the author's view, not necessarily fact |
| **claims** | partisan + sceptical | author asserts it; we don't fully accept |
| **maintains** | partisan + persistent | author sticks to this view |
| **suggests** | weak | hint, possibility |
| **implies** | weak + indirect | not stated directly |
| **indicates** | weak + neutral | data points this way |
| **proposes** | tentative | offers a hypothesis |
| **acknowledges** | concessive | author admits something |
| **questions** | sceptical | author challenges |
| **rejects** | strong negative | author dismisses |

Patterns:

> *Smith (2020) **argues that** the model under-fits the data.*
> *The graph **clearly demonstrates** the trend.*
> *Recent evidence **suggests** a different mechanism.*
> *The author **acknowledges** the limitations of the study.*
> *Brown (2018) **questions** whether the policy was effective.*

Watch the strength carefully. *Smith demonstrates* claims Smith **proved** something. *Smith suggests* claims Smith **hinted** at it. Mismatching is a typical C1 error.

> ❌ *Smith demonstrates the policy might work.* (`demonstrates` is too strong for *might*)
> ✅ *Smith suggests the policy might work.*

## 3 · Hedging — softening your own claims

Academic English is allergic to overclaiming. Hedging language reduces certainty without abandoning the point.

| Hedge | Effect | Example |
|---|---|---|
| *appears to / seems to* | distances from the claim | *The data **appear to support** the hypothesis.* |
| *tends to* | general tendency, not law | *Younger users **tend to prefer** mobile interfaces.* |
| *suggests / indicates* | reduces strength | *The trend **suggests** a slowdown.* |
| *typically / generally / usually* | allows exceptions | *The system **typically responds** within seconds.* |
| *one / it is reasonable to* | distancing | ***One might argue** that …* |
| *modal of probability* | allows uncertainty | *This **may indicate** a shift in policy.* |

Compare:

> Strong: *Climate change **causes** species loss.*
> Hedged: *Climate change **appears to contribute** to species loss in many ecosystems.*

Hedging is not weakness — it is **calibration**. A C1 writer dials confidence up or down to match the evidence.

## 4 · Rhetorical negative questions — for persuasion

A negative question (*Don't you …? · Doesn't it …?*) can be a piece of rhetoric. The form is a question; the function is to **manufacture agreement** before the speaker delivers the punchline.

> ***Don't you just hate** rush-hour traffic? Then you'll love our new express service.*
> ***Doesn't every parent want** the best for their child?*
> ***Don't we all know** what comes next?*

The expected answer is *yes*, even though the form is negative. Compare with a literal question:

| Literal | Rhetorical |
|---|---|
| *Do you hate rush-hour traffic?* (genuinely asking) | *Don't you just hate rush-hour traffic?* (taking the answer for granted) |
| *Do you think it's fascinating?* (asking) | *Doesn't it sound fascinating?* (expecting yes) |

This pattern fuels advertising, opinion writing, and persuasive speech. In **academic** writing, use it sparingly — overusing rhetoric weakens the prose.

---

## 5 · Opinion-checking negative questions

Softer cousin of the persuasion form: you state an opinion and invite the listener to confirm it.

> *Doesn't it **sound** wonderful?*
> *Don't we all **want** more time?*
> *Don't you **think** that style suits her?*

These work like soft tags — they make a statement and politely seek agreement in one breath. They are conversational; not for academic prose. Use *One might argue …* or *It seems reasonable to suggest …* instead in formal writing.

## 6 · Cleft sentences with `it is X who/that …`

A **cleft** (literally, *split*) sentence rearranges a normal sentence to put **emphasis** on one element.

| Plain | Cleft |
|---|---|
| *Smith published the paper.* | ***It is Smith who** publishes the paper.* |
| *The graph shows the trend.* | ***It is the graph that** shows the trend.* |
| *I want a coffee.* | ***What I want is** a coffee.* (pseudo-cleft) |

The cleft uses the **present simple** of `be` (*it is*) when the focus is currently true.

> ***It is the data that **decides**, not opinion.*
> ***What this study shows** is a clear correlation.*
> ***It is in chapter three that** the author **states** her main argument.*

Cleft sentences are common in academic prose to draw attention or contrast:

> ***It is not the cost** that **matters**, **but the principle**.*

## 7 · Fronting + present simple

Move an adverbial or object to the front of the sentence for emphasis. The verb stays in the present simple.

| Plain | Fronted |
|---|---|
| *The author makes this point clearly in chapter three.* | ***In chapter three, the author makes** this point clearly.* |
| *Such cases occur rarely.* | ***Rarely do** such cases **occur**.* (with inversion) |
| *Two themes dominate the discussion.* | ***Dominating the discussion are** two themes.* |

Some fronted adverbials trigger **subject-auxiliary inversion** — they pull the helper before the subject:

| Trigger | Effect | Example |
|---|---|---|
| *Rarely* | inversion | *Rarely **do** we **see** such clarity.* |
| *Seldom* | inversion | *Seldom **does** he **make** mistakes.* |
| *Hardly ever* | inversion | *Hardly ever **does** the system **fail**.* |
| *Only then / only when* | inversion | *Only then **do** we **understand** the cost.* |
| *Not until / not since* | inversion | *Not until now **do** we **see** the truth.* |

Fronting + inversion is a **formal** device. In casual writing it sounds pompous.

## 8 · Generic statements with `one` and `you`

Two ways to make general statements about people in general:

| Pronoun | Register | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **one** | formal, written | ***One does not argue** with success.* |
| **you** (generic) | conversational | ***You don't argue** with success.* |
| **they** (generic) | informal, vague | *They say honesty is the best policy.* |

In academic writing, prefer `one` (or rephrase with the passive). In conversation, generic `you` is natural.

> *In London, **one finds** every cuisine.* (formal)
> *In London, **you find** every cuisine.* (neutral)
> *In London, every cuisine **is found**.* (passive — also academic)

## 9 · Common C1 mistakes

| ❌ Wrong | ✅ Right |
|---|---|
| *Smith demonstrates the policy might work.* | Smith **suggests** the policy might work. |
| *The data demonstrates strongly that …* (formal) | The data **demonstrate** strongly that … (`data` plural in formal use) |
| *It is the cost what matters.* | It is the cost **that** matters. |
| *Rarely we see such clarity.* | **Rarely do we see** such clarity. |
| *Don't you just hates traffic?* | Don't you just **hate** traffic? |
| *Doesn't it sounds great?* | **Doesn't it sound** great? |
| *One do not argue with success.* | **One does not argue** with success. |
| *I think one's right.* (in academic prose) | (use *one* only as generic, not as a contraction substitute) |

2. Take three of your own opinionated sentences and **hedge** them.
3. Write three rhetorical negative questions for an opinion piece.
4. Convert three plain sentences into cleft sentences (*It is X that …*).
