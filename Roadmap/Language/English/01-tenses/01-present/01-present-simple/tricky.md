# Present simple — tricky rules

> The edge cases. Where the rules **bend**, where two correct forms exist, where dialect or register changes everything. If a sentence sounds wrong but you can't say why, the answer is probably here.

## 1 · Stative verbs that *can* go continuous

A1–B1 lessons say "stative verbs never take the continuous." That's a useful first-pass rule, but in real English several stative verbs **shift meaning** when continuous. Knowing when you can break the rule is a C1 / C2 skill.

| Verb | Stative (present simple) | Dynamic (present continuous) |
|---|---|---|
| **think** | *I **think** you're right.* (= I have an opinion) | *I'**m thinking** about my holiday.* (= I'm actively considering) |
| **have** | *I **have** a headache.* (= I possess) | *I'**m having** a headache.* (= I'm experiencing it now) — rare in BrE |
| **see** | *I **see** what you mean.* (= I understand) | *I'**m seeing** the doctor tomorrow.* (= meeting) |
| **smell** | *Roses **smell** good.* (= they have an odour) | *I'**m smelling** the soup.* (= I'm sniffing it) |
| **taste** | *This **tastes** sweet.* (= it has a taste) | *The chef **is tasting** the soup.* (= sampling it) |
| **feel** | *I **feel** sick.* (= state) | *I'**m feeling** much better.* (= experiencing improvement) |
| **be** | *He **is** rude.* (= permanent trait) | *He'**s being** rude.* (= behaving rudely now) |
| **love** | *I **love** chocolate.* (= general) | *I'**m loving** this song.* (= enjoying it now, very informal — *McDonald's slogan*) |
| **understand** | *I **understand** the rule.* (= I get it) | *I'**m understanding** the rule slowly.* (= gradual progress) |
| **expect** | *I **expect** he's late.* (= probability) | *We'**re expecting** a baby.* (= anticipating) |

The shift is always: **state → temporary action**. If you can rephrase the sentence with *right now* or *currently*, the continuous is fine.

## 2 · `Be` is the great exception

`Be` does almost everything differently from other verbs.

| Other verbs | `Be` |
|---|---|
| Negative needs `do/does`: *He doesn't work.* | *He **isn't** here.* — direct `not` |
| Question needs `do/does`: *Does he work?* | ***Is** he here?* — direct inversion |
| Adverb goes before main verb: *I **always** read.* | Adverb after `be`: *I **am always** late.* |
| Continuous needs continuous form | `Being` exists but means **behaving**, not the state |

A learner who treats `be` like other verbs will produce the most consistent error pattern in English:

> ❌ *Does he be tired?* / *He doesn't be ready.*
> ✅ *Is he tired?* / *He isn't ready.*

## 3 · `Have` vs `have got` — BrE / AmE difference

Both mean "to possess".

| 🇬🇧 BrE preference | 🇺🇸 AmE preference |
|---|---|
| *I've got a car.* | *I have a car.* |
| *Have you got a minute?* | *Do you have a minute?* |
| *She hasn't got a phone.* | *She doesn't have a phone.* |

`Have got` is more common in BrE conversation; `have` (with `do/does`) is more common in AmE. Both are correct in either dialect — register is the difference.

**Watch out:** in writing and especially in academic register, plain `have` is preferred everywhere.

> ✅ *The molecule **has** three carbon atoms.* (academic)
> ⚠️ *The molecule's got three carbon atoms.* (too informal for academic prose)

## 4 · `Data` — singular or plural?

| Register | Treatment | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Academic / formal | plural | *The data **show** a clear trend.* |
| Casual / business | singular | *The data **shows** a clear trend.* |

`Data` was historically a Latin plural (singular *datum*). Formal academic English keeps the plural agreement; everyday English has shifted to treating it as a singular mass noun. Both are now accepted; the choice signals register. Be consistent across the document.

The same applies to **media · criteria · phenomena**:

| Singular | Plural | Casual treatment |
|---|---|---|
| medium | media | *The media **is** biased.* (now common, though *are* is technically correct) |
| criterion | criteria | *The criteria **are** strict.* (formal) / *The criteria **is** strict.* (informal, debated) |
| phenomenon | phenomena | *These phenomena **are** rare.* (always plural in formal writing) |

## 5 · Collective nouns — *the team is* vs *the team are*

Nouns like `team`, `family`, `staff`, `government`, `committee`, `audience` can be singular **or** plural, depending on whether you mean the group as a unit or as members.

| Treatment | Use | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Singular | the group as a unit | *The team **is** in good shape.* (one organisation) |
| Plural (BrE more common) | the members individually | *The team **are** wearing red shirts.* (each player) |

In AmE, the singular is more common in both senses. In BrE, the plural is preferred when the members' individual actions are in focus.

> 🇬🇧 *The government **are** divided on the issue.* (members differ)
> 🇺🇸 *The government **is** divided on the issue.*

## 6 · Subject-verb agreement traps

### 6.1 · The phrase looks plural but the head is singular

The verb agrees with the **head** of the subject phrase, not what's nearest.

> ❌ *The **list** of items **are** on the desk.*
> ✅ *The **list** of items **is** on the desk.* (head = `list`)

> ❌ *One **of** the keys **are** missing.*
> ✅ *One of the keys **is** missing.* (head = `one`)

### 6.2 · `Each / every / either / neither + singular`

> *Each student **has** a book.* — singular agreement
> *Every room **needs** cleaning.* — singular
> *Either answer **is** acceptable.* — singular
> *Neither option **works**.* — singular

### 6.3 · `None` — flexible

> *None of the students **is / are** here.* — both accepted; *are* now more common.

### 6.4 · Nouns that look plural but aren't

| Noun | Treatment |
|---|---|
| **news** | singular — *The news **is** good.* |
| **physics, mathematics, economics, politics** (subjects) | singular — *Physics **is** my favourite.* |
| **measles, diabetes** (diseases) | singular |
| **billiards, darts** (games) | singular |
| **United States, the Netherlands** | singular |

### 6.5 · Nouns that look singular but aren't

| Noun | Treatment |
|---|---|
| **trousers, scissors, glasses, jeans, pyjamas** | plural — *My trousers **are** dirty.* |
| **police** | plural — *The police **are** here.* |
| **people** | plural — *People **say** strange things.* |
| **cattle** | plural — *The cattle **graze** in the field.* |

## 7 · Subject questions vs object questions

When `who`, `what`, `which` is the **subject**, drop the helper. When it's the **object**, keep the helper.

| Subject question (no helper) | Object question (with helper) |
|---|---|
| *Who **knows** the answer?* | *Who **does** he **know**?* |
| *What **happens** next?* | *What **does** she **want**?* |
| *Which **costs** more?* | *Which **does** he **prefer**?* |

If you can imagine the answer slot replacing the question word at the front, it's a subject question.

> *Who knows the answer?* → *Mary knows the answer.* — front replacement, subject question
> *Who do you know?* → *I know Mary.* — back replacement, object question

## 8 · `Used to` — past habit, not present

`Used to + verb` describes a past habit — *I used to smoke* (= I did, but I don't now).

This is **not** present simple, despite the surface form. You **cannot** say *I use to smoke* meaning a current habit. To say a current habit, just use the present simple: *I smoke* — or, if you mean a typical pattern, *I usually smoke* / *I tend to smoke*.

> ❌ *I use to drink coffee every morning.* (meaning: I do this now)
> ✅ *I drink coffee every morning.* / *I usually drink coffee in the morning.*

---

## 9 · `Be used to` and `get used to` — totally different

Don't confuse *used to + verb* (past habit) with *be / get used to + noun / -ing* (familiar with).

| Pattern | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| *I **used to live** in Paris.* | past habit (not now) | I lived there before. |
| *I **am used to living** in Paris.* | familiar with | I find it normal now. |
| *I **am getting used to living** here.* | adjusting | becoming familiar. |

The verb after `be / get used to` is in `-ing`, not the base form.

> ❌ *I am used to live here.*
> ✅ *I am used to **living** here.*

## 10 · Stative verb + perception — a sneaky pattern

Some perception verbs (`see`, `hear`, `smell`, `feel`, `taste`) are stative, but they pair naturally with **`can`** in the present simple to express a sensation happening now.

> *I **can see** the lights.* (better than *I see the lights* in BrE)
> *I **can hear** music.* (better than *I hear music*)
> *I **can smell** something burning.*

In American English, the bare present simple (*I see the lights · I hear music*) is also natural. In British English, the *can* version is very common in conversation.

## 11 · Headlines bend the rule

Newspaper headlines compress past, present, and future events into the present simple. This is a register convention, not a grammar rule.

> *Plane **crashes** in mountains.* (= it crashed)
> *PM **resigns** over scandal.* (= she has resigned)
> *Storm **hits** coast tomorrow.* (= will hit)

Don't import this into normal writing.

> ❌ *Yesterday a plane crashes in the mountains.*
> ✅ *Yesterday a plane **crashed** in the mountains.*

## 12 · Quick BrE / AmE differences

| Pattern | 🇬🇧 BrE | 🇺🇸 AmE |
|---|---|---|
| Possess | *I **have got** a car.* | *I **have** a car.* |
| Need + something | *Do you **need** anything?* | *Do you **need** anything?* (same) |
| Suggest + that-clause | *I suggest that he **be** on time.* (subjunctive) or *I suggest that he **should be** on time.* | *I suggest that he **be** on time.* (subjunctive more usual) |
| Group nouns | *The team **are** winning.* | *The team **is** winning.* |
| `Got` vs `gotten` | *I've got a question.* | *I've got* (have) **and** *I've gotten* (received/become) |

## 13 · Pronunciation oddities

Spelling and sound do not always match. The third-person `-s` is pronounced three different ways depending on the verb's last sound.

| Verb ending | `-s` is pronounced | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| voiceless consonant: `p, t, k, f, θ` | /s/ | *stops · works · likes · laughs* |
| voiced consonant: `b, d, g, v, m, n, ŋ, l, r` or vowel | /z/ | *runs · rides · sings · stays · plays* |
| sibilant: `s, z, ʃ, ʒ, tʃ, dʒ` | /ɪz/ | *misses · pushes · watches · changes* |

You won't be marked wrong for this in writing, but it makes a difference in **listening** comprehension and **fluency**.

## 14 · The "always + present continuous" complaint pattern

A surprising present-continuous use that learners confuse with the present simple:

> *He'**s always interrupting** me!* (complaint — happens too often)
> *She'**s constantly losing** her keys.* (frustration)

This is **not** a habit description (*He always interrupts me* would be neutral). The present continuous + *always / constantly / forever* signals **annoyance**.

| Tone | Form | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Neutral habit | present simple | *He **always interrupts** me.* |
| Complaint about excessive habit | present continuous | *He'**s always interrupting** me!* |

If a sentence with *always* in the present simple sounds neutral but you wanted to vent — switch to continuous.

## 15 · Cheat-sheet: when *not* to use the present simple

| Situation | Wrong (present simple) | Right |
|---|---|---|
| Action happening right now | *I read a book right now.* | *I am reading a book right now.* |
| Personal future plan | *I meet Sara tomorrow.* | *I am meeting Sara tomorrow.* |
| Recent past with present relevance | *I see the film yesterday.* | *I have seen the film.* / *I saw the film yesterday.* |
| Action in progress | *He cooks dinner now.* | *He is cooking dinner now.* |
| Temporary situation | *I live with my parents this week.* | *I am living with my parents this week.* |
| Past habit | *I smoke ten years ago.* | *I used to smoke ten years ago.* |
