# Present simple — find the errors

> Each numbered sentence below has **exactly one grammatical error**. Identify it, fix it, then expand the answer key to check.

## A1 — 8 exercises

1. He live in London with his family.
2. She have two brothers.
3. They works at a hospital.
4. I am work in a school.
5. He doesn't likes coffee.
6. Do she speak English?
7. Does she likes the new film?
8. She no like fish.

<details>
<summary>Show A1 answer key</summary>

1. He **lives** in London with his family. — third-person singular needs `-s`.
2. She **has** two brothers. — `have` becomes `has` for he/she/it.
3. They **work** at a hospital. — no `-s` for plural subjects.
4. I **work** in a school. — no `am` before a present-simple verb.
5. He **doesn't like** coffee. — only one `-s`; it goes on `does`, not on the main verb.
6. **Does** she speak English? — third person needs `does`, not `do`.
7. Does she **like** the new film? — main verb stays in base form after `does`.
8. She **doesn't like** fish. — English negates with `don't / doesn't`, not `no`.

</details>

## A2 — 10 exercises

1. Where do he live?
2. How does she spells her name?
3. If you will come, I will help you.
4. I am thinking you are right.
5. Why you don't take a taxi?
6. Do you know where is the station?
7. She studys medicine.
8. He gos to school by bus.
9. I don't never eat fish.
10. We always are together at lunch.

<details>
<summary>Show A2 answer key</summary>

1. Where **does** he live? — third person needs `does`.
2. How does she **spell** her name? — base form after `does`.
3. If you **come**, I will help you. — no `will` in the `if`-clause of the first conditional.
4. I **think** you are right. — `think` is stative when it means "have an opinion".
5. **Why don't you** take a taxi? — auxiliary `don't` comes before the subject in this pattern.
6. Do you know **where the station is**? — embedded questions use statement order.
7. She **studies** medicine. — `-y` after a consonant becomes `-ies`.
8. He **goes** to school by bus. — verbs ending in `-o` add `-es`.
9. I **never** eat fish. — `never` is already negative; don't double up.
10. We **are always** together at lunch. — frequency adverb goes after `be`.

</details>

## B1 — 10 exercises

1. I am understanding the rule now.
2. He says is she late.
3. I recommend you to stay at the hotel.
4. I apologise to miss the meeting.
5. The train will leave at six tomorrow.
6. Don't you think it's strange, don't you think?
7. Who do likes coffee in this office?
8. Does the students know about the deadline?
9. This bag is belonging to me.
10. The chart show that sales are rising.

<details>
<summary>Show B1 answer key</summary>

1. I **understand** the rule now. — `understand` is stative; use the present simple.
2. He **says she is** late. — reported clauses keep statement word order.
3. I **recommend you stay** at the hotel. (or *I recommend staying at the hotel*.) — `recommend` does **not** take *object + to + verb*.
4. I **apologise for missing** the meeting. — `apologise for` takes `-ing`, not `to`.
5. The train **leaves** at six tomorrow. — schedules use the present simple, not `will`.
6. It's strange, **don't you think**? — the tag goes once, at the end.
7. Who **likes** coffee in this office? — `who` as subject takes no helper.
8. **Do** the students know about the deadline? — plural subject takes `do`, not `does`.
9. This bag **belongs** to me. — `belong` is stative; never continuous.
10. The chart **shows** that sales are rising. — third-person `-s` on `shows`.

</details>

## B2 — 8 exercises

1. The hero meets a stranger and they took the train together.
2. Not only they save time, but also reduce errors.
3. I disagree to the new system.
4. I advise that you to take a coat.
5. He claims to see the accident yesterday.
6. She denies to take the money.
7. I welcome to the decision.
8. Hamlet meets the ghost and decided to take revenge.

<details>
<summary>Show B2 answer key</summary>

1. The hero **meets** a stranger and they **take** the train together. — historical present requires consistency.
2. **Not only do they save** time, but they also reduce errors. — fronted *not only* triggers subject-auxiliary inversion.
3. I disagree **with** the new system. — `disagree with`, not `disagree to`.
4. I advise **you to take** a coat. (or *I advise that you take a coat*.) — `advise` takes either object + to-infinitive or a `that`-clause, not both.
5. He **claims that he saw** / **claims to have seen** the accident yesterday. — present-perfect infinitive (`to have seen`) for past reference under a present reporting verb, or full clause.
6. She **denies taking** the money. — `deny + -ing`, not `deny + to + verb`.
7. I **welcome** the decision. — `welcome` takes a direct object; no `to`.
8. Hamlet **meets** the ghost and **decides** to take revenge. — stay in the historical present.

</details>

## C1 — 8 exercises

1. Smith demonstrates the policy might work.
2. The data demonstrates strongly that there is a correlation.
3. It is the cost what matters here.
4. Rarely we see such clarity in academic writing.
5. Don't you just hates rush-hour traffic?
6. Doesn't it sounds wonderful?
7. One do not argue with success.
8. The graph illustrates that the trend.

<details>
<summary>Show C1 answer key</summary>

1. Smith **suggests** the policy might work. — `demonstrates` is too strong for *might*; mismatch of strength.
2. The data **demonstrate** strongly that there is a correlation. — formal academic English treats `data` as plural.
3. It is the cost **that** matters here. — cleft sentences use `that` (or `which`), not `what`.
4. **Rarely do we see** such clarity in academic writing. — fronted negative adverb triggers inversion.
5. Don't you just **hate** rush-hour traffic? — base form after `do/does`.
6. **Doesn't it sound** wonderful? — base form after `doesn't`.
7. **One does not argue** with success. — `one` takes singular agreement (`does`).
8. The graph **illustrates the trend**. (or *The graph **shows that the trend** is rising*.) — `illustrate` takes a noun phrase, not a `that`-clause without a verb.

</details>

## C2 — 7 exercises

1. I do really think she's wrong.
2. Here is coming the bus.
3. I hereby agree to lunch on Friday.
4. Smith demonstrates that the data shows a slight rise.
5. The president walks into the room and announced the decision.
6. She do work hard, you know.
7. Were he to come, I would told him.

<details>
<summary>Show C2 answer key</summary>

1. I **really do** think she's wrong. — emphatic `do` follows the standard adverb order; *really* before `do`.
2. **Here comes** the bus. — fixed inversion after `here / there`; no `is`.
3. I'd **love to have** lunch on Friday. — `hereby` is a legal-formal performative; over-formal for a casual lunch.
4. Smith **shows** / **suggests** that the data shows a slight rise. — *demonstrates* clashes with the hedged *shows a slight rise*; downgrade to match.
5. The president **walks** into the room and **announces** the decision. (historical present consistent) **or** *walked … announced …* (past simple consistent). Don't mix.
6. She **does** work hard, you know. — emphatic `do` is `does` for third-person singular.
7. Were he to come, I would **tell** him. — base form after *would*, not the past tense.

</details>

## All-levels mixed quiz — 10 exercises

For practice across the whole present-simple terrain. Errors come from any level.

1. He works in a hospital and his wife work in a school.
2. Does she works on weekends?
3. The data confirms strongly that the hypothesis is correct. (formal academic register)
4. I am loving this song right now.
5. Not only does he work hard, but he also working overtime.
6. Where does he lives?
7. The chart shows that sales were rising over the last quarter.
8. I am used to live in Tashkent.
9. Plane crashes in mountains yesterday and twenty people are killed.
10. If she will arrive late, please tell her to wait.

<details>
<summary>Show mixed-quiz answer key</summary>

1. He works in a hospital and his wife **works** in a school. — third-person `-s` on `works`.
2. Does she **work** on weekends? — base form after `does`.
3. The data **confirm** strongly that the hypothesis is correct. — formal academic plural.
4. I **love** this song. — `love` is normally stative. (*"I'm loving it"* is a McDonald's-style colloquialism — fine in casual speech, not in writing.)
5. Not only does he work hard, but he also **works** overtime. — parallel structure; both verbs in the present simple.
6. Where does he **live**? — base form after `does`.
7. The chart shows that sales **have been rising** / **rose** / **were rising** over the last quarter. — past time reference; the reporting verb stays present, but the reported clause shifts to past or present-perfect.
8. I am used to **living** in Tashkent. — `be used to + -ing`.
9. **A plane crashed** in the mountains yesterday and twenty people **were killed**. — newspaper-headline present is for headlines only; the body of the article uses past tense.
10. If she **arrives** late, please tell her to wait. — no `will` in the `if`-clause.

</details>
