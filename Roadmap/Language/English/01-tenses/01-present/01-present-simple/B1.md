# Present simple — B1

> Now that the form is automatic, the present simple becomes a **register tool**: how to report, how to schedule, how to invite agreement, how to talk about thoughts in writing.

[siblings: A1](A1.md) · [A2](A2.md) · [B2](B2.md) · [C1](C1.md) · [C2](C2.md) · [tricky](tricky.md) · [find errors](find-errors.md)

---

## 1 · What's new at B1

By B1 you have the form mastered. The level adds:

1. A wider family of **stative verbs**.
2. **Reporting** what others say or what evidence shows.
3. **Speech-act verbs** — verbs that *do* the act when you say them.
4. **Schedules and timetables** for fixed-future events.
5. **Tag-style invitations to agree** — *don't you think?*, *don't you agree?*
6. **Subject vs object questions** with `who`, `what`, `which`.

---

## 2 · Wider stative verbs

At A2 you knew `think`, `hope`, `know`, `want`, `like`. At B1, add:

| Verb | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **remember** | recall | *I **don't remember** her name.* |
| **understand** | grasp | *I **understand** what you mean.* |
| **believe** | think it's true | *I **believe** he is honest.* |
| **realise** | suddenly see | *I **realise** how lucky I am.* |
| **mean** | intend to say | *What **does this word mean**?* |
| **recognise** | identify | *I **recognise** that face.* |
| **agree / disagree** | share opinion | *I **agree** with you.* |
| **suppose** | guess, assume | *I **suppose** you're right.* |
| **doubt** | not believe | *I **doubt** it.* |
| **wonder** | be curious | *I **wonder** why.* |
| **own** | possess | *They **own** three flats.* |
| **belong** | be the property of | *That book **belongs** to me.* |
| **contain** | hold inside | *The box **contains** old letters.* |
| **consist of** | be made up of | *Water **consists of** hydrogen and oxygen.* |
| **involve** | include as part | *The job **involves** travelling.* |

These verbs almost never appear in the continuous form when they have their stative meaning.

> ✅ *I **don't remember** the title.*
> ❌ *I'm not remembering the title.*
> ✅ *This bag **belongs** to me.*
> ❌ *This bag is belonging to me.*

(See [tricky.md](tricky.md) for the cases where these verbs *can* go continuous with a different meaning.)

## 3 · Reporting what others say or evidence shows

Use the present simple with verbs like `say`, `show`, `mean`, `tell` when the report is **general or still valid**.

| Pattern | Use | Example |
|---|---|---|
| *X says (that) + clause* | repeat someone's words | *He **says** he's busy this week.* |
| *X shows (that) + clause* | data or visuals reveal | *The graph **shows** sales are rising.* |
| *X tells me (that) + clause* | information given to me | *My doctor **tells me** I should rest.* |
| *X means (that) + clause* | explain | *That **means** we're late.* |

> *The article **says** the prices will fall.*
> *The chart **shows** how the population grew.*
> *He always **tells me** I work too hard.*

Use the **past** simple (*said*, *showed*) only for one specific past moment of speech: *He **said** he was tired* (yesterday, once). Use the **present** simple when the source is still authoritative or the claim is still being made.

In reported clauses, **keep statement word order** — no inversion:

> ✅ *He **says (that) she is** late.*
> ❌ *He says is she late.*

## 4 · Speech-act verbs — verbs that *perform* the act

Some verbs **do** the action when you say them. Saying *I apologise* is itself the apology. These verbs go in the present simple, first person.

| Verb | Pattern | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **apologise** | *for + -ing / for + noun* | *I **apologise** for the delay.* |
| **promise** | *to + verb* | *I **promise** to call you.* |
| **suggest** | *-ing / that-clause* | *I **suggest** taking the early train.* |
| **recommend** | *-ing / that-clause* | *I **recommend** the soup.* |
| **agree** | *to + verb / with + noun / that-clause* | *I **agree** with you.* |
| **invite** | *object + to + verb* | *I **invite** you to dinner.* |
| **accept** | *noun / that-clause* | *I **accept** your offer.* |
| **thank** | *object + for + -ing* | *I **thank** you for your help.* |

> *I **promise** I'll be on time.*
> *I **recommend staying** at the hotel near the park.*
> *We **thank** you for your patience.*

Watch the patterns. *Recommend* takes `-ing` or a `that`-clause, **not** *object + to + verb*:

> ❌ *I recommend you to stay.*
> ✅ *I recommend you stay.* (or *I recommend staying.*)

## 5 · Schedules and timetables — present simple for the *fixed* future

When something is on a published schedule — a train, a flight, a film, a class — English uses the present simple even though the event is in the future.

> *The train **leaves** at 06:30.*
> *Our flight **arrives** at 21:45 tomorrow.*
> *The film **starts** in ten minutes.*
> *Class **finishes** at four.*
> *The shop **opens** on Monday.*

This is **not** for personal plans — for those, use the present continuous (*I am meeting Sara tomorrow*) or *be going to* (*I'm going to call her*).

| Use this … | When … |
|---|---|
| present simple | the time is fixed by a schedule, timetable, programme |
| present continuous | you have a personal arrangement |
| be going to | you have decided / intend to do it |

## 6 · *Don't you think?*, *don't you agree?*

A friendly tag attached to a statement to invite agreement.

> *It's a great idea, **don't you think**?*
> *We should leave now, **don't you agree**?*
> *He works too hard, **don't you think**?*

Pattern: full statement + comma + tag. The tag is **invariable** — it is always *don't you think* / *don't you agree*, regardless of what subject the statement uses.

You can attach `so` for shorter agreement-checking:

> *— Maybe she's right.*
> *— **I think so**.*
> *— Don't **you think so** too?*

Don't double the tag at both ends:

> ❌ *Don't you think it's strange, don't you think?*
> ✅ *It's strange, don't you think?*

## 7 · Subject vs object questions

When `who`, `what`, or `which` is the **subject** of the question, drop the helper:

> *Who **knows** the answer?*
> *What **happens** next?*
> *Which **costs** more, A or B?*

When they are the **object**, keep the helper:

> *Who **does she love**?*
> *What **do you want**?*
> *Which **does he prefer**?*

A good test: try answering. If the answer slot replaces the question word at the start, it's a subject question. If it replaces the word at the end, it's an object question.

> *Who knows the answer?* → *Mary knows the answer.* (subject question — answer goes at the front)
> *Who do you know?* → *I know Mary.* (object question — answer goes at the end)

---

## 8 · Wider question subjects

At A2 you mostly asked questions with `you`. At B1 you handle any subject — long noun phrases, possessives, abstract nouns.

> ***Does the next train** go to London?*
> ***Does your sister** speak English?*
> ***Do all the students** pay the same fee?*
> ***Does the system** work properly?*

Make sure the helper agrees with the **head** of the subject noun phrase:

> *Does **the new student** know the timetable?* (singular head — `does`)
> *Do **the new students** know the timetable?* (plural head — `do`)
> *Does **everyone** agree?* (`everyone` is grammatically singular — `does`)

## 9 · Common B1 mistakes

| ❌ Wrong | ✅ Right |
|---|---|
| *I am understanding the rule.* | I **understand** the rule. |
| *He says is she late.* | He **says she is** late. |
| *I recommend you to stay.* | I **recommend you stay** / **recommend staying**. |
| *I apologise to miss the class.* | I **apologise for missing** the class. |
| *The train will leave at six.* (timetable) | The train **leaves** at six. |
| *Don't you think it's strange, don't you think?* | It's strange, **don't you think**? |
| *Who do likes coffee?* | Who **likes** coffee? |
| *Which do costs more?* | Which **costs** more? |
| *Does the students know?* | **Do** the students know? |
| *I'm wondering if you can help.* (real wondering) | I **wonder** if you can help. |

## 10 · Practice

1. Take a chart, table, or graph you've seen recently. Write three sentences using *The chart **shows** that …*
2. Apologise for three things using *I apologise for …* with `-ing`.
3. Make three speech-act sentences (*I promise / suggest / recommend / agree …*).
4. Write three schedule sentences (a train, a film, a meeting).
5. Find a statement you agree with and append *don't you think?* twice.
6. Write five subject questions with `who`, `what`, `which` (no `do`/`does`).
7. Write five object questions with the same words (with `do`/`does`).

When done, move to [B2](B2.md).
